# Cooperatives

Member-owned, democratically governed business structures in Canada, with focus on BC Cooperative Association Act.

## Entity Overview

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Primary Use** | Member-owned business serving members' needs |
| **Liability** | Limited (cooperative is separate legal entity) |
| **Taxation** | Patronage dividends deductible; members taxed |
| **Governance** | One member, one vote (typically) |
| **Duration** | Perpetual existence |

## Cooperative Principles

International Cooperative Alliance principles:

1. **Voluntary and open membership**
2. **Democratic member control**
3. **Member economic participation**
4. **Autonomy and independence**
5. **Education, training, and information**
6. **Cooperation among cooperatives**
7. **Concern for community**

## Types of Cooperatives

### Worker Cooperatives

**Ownership**: Employee-members own and govern
**Purpose**: Provide employment; share profits with workers
**Governance**: Democratic (one worker, one vote)
**Examples**: Restaurants, design studios, tech companies, trades

**Benefits**:
- Aligned incentives (workers = owners)
- Democratic workplace
- Profit sharing
- Resilience (members invested in success)

### Consumer Cooperatives

**Ownership**: Customer-members own and govern
**Purpose**: Provide goods/services to members
**Governance**: One member, one vote
**Examples**: Credit unions, food co-ops, REI (in US)

**Benefits**:
- Members control what they purchase
- Profits returned to members (patronage dividends)
- Often values-driven (local, sustainable)

### Producer/Marketing Cooperatives

**Ownership**: Producer-members (farmers, artisans)
**Purpose**: Collectively market members' products
**Governance**: One member, one vote
**Examples**: Dairy cooperatives, grain pools, craft collectives

**Benefits**:
- Collective bargaining power
- Shared marketing costs
- Quality standards

### Housing Cooperatives

**Ownership**: Resident-members own shares, not units
**Purpose**: Provide affordable housing
**Governance**: Democratic (member board)
**Examples**: Non-profit housing co-ops throughout Canada

**Special Considerations**:
- No individual ownership of units
- Members hold occupancy rights
- Cannot sell unit for profit (non-equity co-ops)
- Equity co-ops allow appreciation

### Multi-Stakeholder Cooperatives

**Ownership**: Multiple stakeholder classes
**Purpose**: Serve diverse stakeholders together
**Governance**: Each class may have representation
**Examples**: Community-owned enterprises, care co-ops

**Structure**:
- Worker members
- Consumer members
- Producer members
- Community/investor members
- Each class may have board seats

## [BC] BC Cooperative Association Act

### Incorporation Process

1. **Name Approval**: Must include "Cooperative", "Co-operative", "Coop", or "Co-op"
2. **Memorandum**: State cooperative's purposes
3. **Rules**: Governance provisions (like bylaws)
4. **Filing**: Submit to BC Registry
5. **Members**: Minimum 3 incorporating members

### Membership

**Admission**: Must apply; board decides
**Equality**: Generally one member, one vote
**Classes**: Can have different member classes with different rights
**Withdrawal**: Members can withdraw (may have restrictions)
**Expulsion**: For cause, with due process

### Shares and Investment

**Membership Shares**: Required to be a member
- Usually nominal value ($1-$100)
- One share per member (typically)
- Not tradeable
- Redeemable on withdrawal at par (no gain)

**Investment Shares**: Optional, for raising capital
- Can pay dividends
- Can be owned by non-members (depending on rules)
- May have restricted voting or no voting
- Limits on returns (reasonable, not excessive)

### Governance

**Members Meeting**: Supreme authority
- Annual general meeting required
- Special meetings as needed
- Vote on major decisions, elect directors

**Board of Directors**: Manages affairs
- Elected by members
- Fiduciary duties
- Minimum 3 directors

**Officers**: Appointed by board
- Day-to-day management

### Patronage Returns

Profits can be returned to members based on patronage (usage), not investment.

**Calculation**: Based on member's transactions with co-op
**Form**: Cash, additional shares, or credits
**Tax Treatment**: Deductible by co-op; taxable to members

### Federal Cooperative Option

Canada Cooperatives Act (federal) available for:
- Multi-provincial cooperatives
- National operations

Generally similar structure to BC but federal jurisdiction.

## Tax Treatment

### Cooperative Taxation

**Patronage Dividends**:
- Deductible by cooperative when allocated to members
- Taxable to members when received (or allocated)
- Must be allocated based on patronage, not investment

**Corporate Income**:
- Cooperative pays corporate tax on retained earnings
- Rates similar to regular corporations
- May qualify for small business deduction (complex rules)

**Member Taxation**:
- Members include patronage dividends in income
- No special tax rate (taxed at member's rate)

### Comparison to Corporation

| Aspect | Cooperative | CCPC |
|--------|-------------|------|
| Profit distribution | Patronage (by usage) | Dividends (by shares) |
| Distribution taxation | Deductible/income | Non-deductible/dividend credits |
| Capital gains on sale | Limited (shares at par) | Yes (can use LCGE) |
| Governance | Democratic | Plutocratic (by shares) |

## Worker Cooperative Deep Dive

### Membership Requirements

Typically:
- Probation period (6-12 months)
- Purchase membership share
- Vote of existing members
- Ongoing participation requirements

### Compensation Structures

**Equal Pay**: All workers paid same
**Ratio Limits**: E.g., highest paid â‰¤ 3x lowest
**Market-Based**: Competitive wages within limits
**Hybrid**: Base wage + patronage share

### Governance Best Practices

**Decision Types**:
- Operational (manager/committee)
- Policy (board)
- Constitutional (membership)

**Consensus vs Voting**:
- Many co-ops use consensus for major decisions
- Voting as fallback
- Supermajority for key issues

### Financing Challenges

**Traditional Barriers**:
- Banks unfamiliar with co-op model
- No traditional equity to sell
- Limited collateral

**Solutions**:
- Member loans
- Community bonds
- Credit union financing (co-op to co-op)
- Government programs (varies by province)
- Investment shares (where permitted)

## Housing Cooperative Deep Dive

### Equity vs Non-Equity

**Non-Equity Co-op**:
- Member pays housing charge (rent equivalent)
- No appreciation/profit on leaving
- Often involves government subsidy
- True affordable housing

**Equity Co-op**:
- Member purchases share at market value
- Share appreciates with market
- Can profit on sale
- Limited equity caps appreciation

### Governance

**Board**: Elected from residents
**Committees**: Maintenance, finance, social, etc.
**Participation**: Often required (e.g., 4 hours/month)

### Financial Model

**Housing Charge**: Covers operating costs, reserve fund
**Reserve Fund**: Required for major repairs
**Operating Budget**: No profit motive

## Common Scenarios

### Tech Worker Cooperative

**Structure**: Worker co-op under BC Cooperative Association Act
- All employees are member-owners
- Democratic governance (one person, one vote)
- Profits distributed as patronage based on hours/contribution
- Probation period before membership

**Benefits**:
- Aligned incentives
- Retention through ownership
- Resilient to market downturns

**Challenges**:
- Slower decision-making
- Difficulty raising external capital
- Buyout complexity for departing members

### Community Food Co-op

**Structure**: Consumer co-op
- Customers buy membership
- Member discount on purchases
- Annual patronage dividend based on purchases
- Democratic control of sourcing, values

**Governance**:
- Member-elected board
- Professional management
- Committees for engagement

### Housing Co-op Development

**Structure**: Non-equity housing co-op
- Members hold occupancy rights
- Housing charges cover costs
- Democratic building management
- Often government-subsidized initially

**Development Process**:
1. Group formation
2. Site acquisition
3. Development financing
4. Construction
5. Occupancy and ongoing management

### Multi-Stakeholder Care Co-op

**Structure**: Multi-stakeholder co-op
- Worker members (caregivers)
- Consumer members (care recipients/families)
- Community members (broader stakeholders)

**Governance**:
- Board seats allocated by class
- Different rights for different classes
- Balance of interests

## Cooperative vs Other Structures

### When to Choose Cooperative

**Good Fit**:
- Democratic values important
- Members will actively participate
- Patronage-based returns make sense
- Long-term community orientation
- Worker ownership desired

**Poor Fit**:
- Need external investment
- Rapid scaling planned
- Exit via sale expected
- One person wants control
- Members won't participate

### Hybrid Possibilities

**Cooperative + Subsidiary Corporation**:
- Co-op owns for-profit subsidiary
- Subsidiary handles external investment
- Co-op maintains democratic control

**Cooperative + Trust**:
- Trust holds co-op shares for future members
- Preserves mission long-term
